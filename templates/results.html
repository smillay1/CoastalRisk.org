Risk factors

Erosion levels
{{ results['erosion'] }}
Relative risk
Erosion describes how quickly the shoreline is retreating over time due to waves, storms, and sediment supply. Higher values indicate faster or more persistent shoreline loss.
Sea-level rise
{{ results['sea_level'] }}
Long-term trend
Sea-level rise reflects projected increases in mean sea level from ocean warming and ice melt. Higher values mean greater potential for chronic flooding and permanent inundation.
Tidal risk
{{ results['tides'] }}
High-water exposure
Tidal risk captures the range and intensity of astronomical tides. Higher values indicate larger tidal ranges and more frequent high-water levels that can worsen flooding.
Geomorphology
{{ results['geomorphology'] }}
Shoreline type
Geomorphology describes the physical shape and material of the coast (rocky cliffs, dunes, barrier islands, wetlands, etc.). Softer, low-lying shorelines are generally more vulnerable than hard, steep, rocky coasts.
Wave climate
{{ results['waves'] }}
Energy at the coast
Wave climate reflects the typical height and energy of waves reaching the shore. Higher values mean stronger wave forces that can drive more erosion and coastal flooding.
Coastal slope
{{ results['slope'] }}
Inland run-up
Coastal slope measures how steeply the land rises from the shoreline inland. Flatter slopes allow water to travel farther inland, increasing the extent of flooding.
